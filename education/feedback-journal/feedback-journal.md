# Feedback Journal

## Why This Matters

This is the file that makes your context stack get smarter over time. Every correction you make teaches AI something it could not learn from your other documents. Over weeks and months, this journal compounds into a set of hard-won rules that dramatically reduce AI friction.

## Corrections

<!-- Things AI consistently gets wrong about you or your domain. State the correction as a clear rule. Format: **[Date]**: [What happened] > [The rule going forward] -->

- **2026-03-10**: AI kept suggesting Agile ceremony templates when I asked about planning processes. > My planning process is not Scrum. Do not default to sprint ceremonies. Ask which framework I am using before suggesting templates.
- **2026-03-18**: AI wrote an executive summary using bullet points and headers. > Executive summaries for my leadership team should be narrative prose, not bullet lists. Save the bullets for appendices.
- **2026-03-25**: AI assumed "roadmap" meant a Gantt chart. > When I say roadmap, I mean a theme-based strategic view grouped by outcomes, not a timeline of features.

## Validated Approaches

<!-- Things AI does well that you want to reinforce. Calling these out ensures good patterns persist. -->

- **2026-03-12**: Claude structured a stakeholder analysis as a 2x2 matrix (influence vs. interest). > This format works perfectly. Use it as the default for stakeholder analysis requests.
- **2026-03-20**: AI drafted meeting agendas with time allocations and decision-needed flags. > Keep doing this. Every agenda should have time boxes and clearly mark which items need a decision.
- **2026-04-01**: Claude used a "current state / desired state / gap / action" framework for my process assessment. > This four-part structure is exactly how I think about operational improvements. Default to it.

## Format Preferences

<!-- Output formats that work vs. ones that do not. Be specific. -->

- **2026-03-14**: AI gave me a 2,000-word analysis when I needed a decision brief. > Unless I ask for depth, default to one page. Lead with the recommendation, then supporting evidence.
- **2026-03-22**: AI used a numbered list for pros and cons. > Use a two-column table for comparison. Numbered lists make it hard to see tradeoffs at a glance.
- **2026-04-02**: AI put caveats and disclaimers throughout a draft. > Put caveats in a single "Risks and Assumptions" section at the end. Do not hedge inline; it weakens the narrative.

## Recurring Requests

<!-- Patterns in what you ask AI to do. Recognizing these helps AI anticipate your needs and prepare better responses. -->

- Weekly executive status updates (every Monday morning)
- Meeting prep briefs: attendee context, open items, proposed agenda
- First-draft stakeholder communications after major decisions
- Process documentation: current state capture before proposing changes

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

<!-- Specific mistakes to never repeat. These are your "never again" rules. -->

- **2026-03-15**: AI rewrote my draft in a completely different voice without being asked. > Never restyle my writing unless I explicitly ask for a tone change. Editing for clarity is fine; changing my voice is not.
- **2026-03-28**: AI suggested reorganizing my team structure as a solution to a process problem. > Do not suggest org changes unless I specifically ask about team structure. Process problems get process solutions first.
- **2026-04-01**: AI added motivational language ("This is an exciting opportunity to...") to an internal memo. > Never use promotional or motivational language in internal documents. State facts, state actions, move on.
