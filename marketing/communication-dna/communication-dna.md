# Communication DNA

## Why This Matters

AI-generated text that does not sound like you gets rejected immediately, no matter how good the content is. This file teaches AI your voice so that drafts, emails, and documents feel like they came from you, not from a machine. The anti-patterns section is especially critical: most people identify what sounds wrong faster than what sounds right.

## Default Register

<!-- Your baseline communication style across most professional contexts. Think of this as your "resting state" for written communication. -->

- **Formality**: Professional but approachable. Never stiff, never slangy.
- **Length**: Concise. Get to the point in the first sentence. Details follow only if needed.
- **Directness**: Direct without being blunt. State the recommendation, then provide reasoning. Do not bury the lead.
- **Warmth**: Warm but not effusive. Acknowledge people briefly, then move to substance.

## Writing Patterns

<!-- Specific habits in how you construct sentences and choose words. These are the fingerprints of your writing. -->

- Short sentences. Rarely more than 20 words.
- Active voice almost exclusively. "We decided" not "A decision was made."
- Plain language over jargon. Say "we need to pick one" not "we need to rationalize our approach."
- Numbered lists for action items. Bullets for context. Never mix them.
- One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph has two ideas, split it.
- Use "I think" and "my read is" to signal opinions vs. facts.

## Format Preferences

<!-- How you structure different types of communication. Cover the formats you use most. -->

**Emails**: Subject line states the ask or decision. First line is the action needed. Supporting context below. Never more than three short paragraphs for routine items.

**Slack/Chat**: One message, complete thought. No "hi" followed by waiting. No walls of text. Link to documents for anything longer than 3 sentences.

**Documents**: Start with a summary or recommendation. Use headers aggressively. Tables over paragraphs for comparisons. End with clear next steps and owners.

**Presentations**: One idea per slide. Minimal text on screen; detail in speaker notes. Lead with the "so what" before the data.

## Context Shifting

<!-- How your communication style changes depending on audience. AI needs to know these shifts to draft appropriately for different recipients. -->

- **To executives**: Shorter. Lead with impact and recommendation. Quantify where possible. No process details unless asked.
- **To direct reports**: More conversational. Include reasoning behind decisions. Ask questions rather than dictate.
- **To peers**: Collaborative tone. "What do you think about..." and "My suggestion would be..." rather than directives.
- **To external partners/vendors**: Slightly more formal. Clear on expectations and timelines. Professional courtesy without over-friendliness.
- **To large audiences (all-hands, announcements)**: Confident but inclusive. "We" language. Acknowledge effort before pivoting to what is next.

## Anti-Patterns

<!-- Things that make you immediately reject AI-generated text. These are your dealbreakers. Be specific. -->

- Corporate buzzwords: "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "move the needle." Delete on sight.
- Passive constructions: "It was determined that..." No. Say who determined it.
- Filler openings: "I hope this email finds you well." Never.
- Over-hedging: "Perhaps we might consider potentially exploring..." Just say what you think.
- Exclamation points in professional writing. One per document maximum, if any.
- Long preambles before getting to the point. The ask goes first.
- Lists of three where two will do. Do not pad for symmetry.

## Signature Moves

<!-- Phrases, structures, or approaches that are distinctly yours. These are the things colleagues would recognize as "that sounds like you." -->

- Starting emails with the decision or request in bold, then context below.
- Using "Here is my read:" to introduce analysis.
- Closing with "Next step:" (singular, specific, with an owner and date).
- Framing trade-offs as "Option A gets us X but costs Y. Option B gets us Z but costs W. I lean toward A because..."
- Using questions to redirect: "What problem are we actually solving here?"
