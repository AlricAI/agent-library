# Collaboration Profile

## Why This Matters

How you work with others is as important as what you know. This file helps AI prepare you for meetings, draft feedback that matches your style, and support team dynamics in a way that feels natural. It also helps AI understand your leadership approach so recommendations fit your management philosophy.

## Team Position

<!-- Your typical role in teams. This may vary by context. Describe your default and when it shifts. -->

Default: Facilitator and connector. I run the process, keep the group aligned, and make sure the right people are heard. I rarely position myself as the authority on content; I position myself as the authority on how we get to a decision.

Shifts to contributor when the topic is in my expert zone (operations, planning, stakeholder management). Shifts to advisor when working with teams I do not directly manage, where I offer perspective but do not own outcomes.

## Collaboration Preferences

<!-- How you prefer to work with others. Synchronous vs. asynchronous, group size, structure level. -->

- **Async by default**: Written communication for status updates, decisions that need documentation, and anything where people need time to think. Do not default to a meeting.
- **Sync for ambiguity**: When a topic is emotionally charged, politically sensitive, or genuinely unclear, get in a room (or on a call). Text creates more problems than it solves in those cases.
- **Small groups**: 3-5 people is the ideal working session. Larger groups are for communication, not collaboration.
- **Structured but not rigid**: Agendas and time-boxes for meetings. Clear outcomes defined upfront. But allow room for the conversation to go where it needs to go.

## Feedback Style

<!-- How you give feedback, how you receive it, and what format works best in each direction. -->

**Giving feedback:**
- Direct and specific. "This section needs a clearer recommendation" not "this could be improved."
- Always tie feedback to impact. Why does this matter? What changes if they fix it?
- Deliver critical feedback privately, never in group settings.
- Lead with what is working before addressing what is not. Not as a formula; because the good parts genuinely matter.

**Receiving feedback:**
- Prefer written feedback I can process on my own time. Do not ambush me in a hallway.
- Be specific. "This did not land well" is not useful. "The executive team felt the timeline was unrealistic because X" is useful.
- I take feedback seriously but not personally. Give it straight.

## Delegation Approach

<!-- What you delegate, what you hold, and how you check in without micromanaging. -->

- **Delegate**: Execution details, first drafts, research, meeting scheduling, routine stakeholder updates.
- **Hold**: Final decisions on priorities, executive communications, anything with organizational risk, relationship management with key stakeholders.
- **Check-in style**: Weekly 1:1s for ongoing work. I ask "what do you need from me?" more than "what is the status?" If someone is blocked, I want to know immediately, not at the next scheduled check-in.
- **Trust by default**: I give full ownership and only pull back if results consistently miss the mark. I would rather course-correct than pre-approve.

## Meeting Philosophy

<!-- What kinds of meetings you value, what you avoid, how you prepare and expect others to prepare. -->

- **Value**: Decision meetings with a clear question to answer. Working sessions where we produce something together. Retrospectives that lead to real changes.
- **Avoid**: Status meetings that could be an email. Meetings without an agenda. Meetings where the decision has already been made and the meeting is theater.
- **Preparation**: I read pre-reads. I expect others to do the same. If nobody reads the pre-read, cancel the meeting and send a summary instead.
- **Facilitation**: I time-box agenda items. I call on quiet voices. I end with "what did we decide and who owns what." Every meeting produces a written outcome within 24 hours.

## Conflict Approach

<!-- How you handle disagreements, what escalation looks like, and your philosophy on productive tension. -->

- **Default response**: Name the disagreement explicitly. "It sounds like we see this differently. Here is what I am hearing from each side." Surface it; do not let it simmer.
- **Productive tension**: Disagreement about ideas is healthy and necessary. I protect it. People should feel safe pushing back on my recommendations.
- **Unproductive conflict**: Personal attacks, passive resistance, or repeated relitigating of settled decisions. I address these privately and directly.
- **Escalation path**: If two parties cannot align after one structured conversation, I bring in the next level of leadership with a clear framing of the disagreement and a recommended path forward. I do not escalate without a recommendation.
- **Resolution priority**: Relationships survive disagreements. I check in with people after hard conversations to make sure we are good.
